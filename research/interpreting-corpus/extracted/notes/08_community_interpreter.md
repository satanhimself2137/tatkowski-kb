# The Community Interpreter: An International Textbook (Bancroft, García Beyaert, Allen, et al.) — Notes
*Pedagogical/ethics textbook. Limited direct cashflow content; value is in market taxonomy, on-assignment ethics, standards landscape, and CPD. Source read: definitions, interpreter-categories, business-ethics, and professional-development sections verified against text.*

## Market taxonomy (useful precision for our positioning)
- **ISP (Interpreting Service Provider)** is the umbrella term. "All freelance interpreters are technically ISPs, but not all ISPs are freelance interpreters." An ISP can be a freelancer, a nonprofit service, a hospital language service, or a commercial language company.
- **Freelance interpreter** = not an employee; a self-employed independent contractor (a.k.a. contract/self-employed interpreter).
- Categories of interpreter in the market:
  - Full-time staff interpreters (salary + benefits).
  - Independent freelancers taking contract jobs from language companies, courts, or nonprofits.
  - Bilingual staff whose primary job isn't interpreting but who interpret ad hoc.
  - Volunteers in hospital/charity/government "language banks".
- Live debate over who counts as a "community interpreter". The pragmatic position (US NCIHC): the real test is whether someone has the qualifications and skills to interpret at a professional level — not their job title. Important because interpreters in less-common languages often can't make a full-time living from it.

## Community vs business/conference interpreting (how the segment differs)
- Community interpreting is predominantly face-to-face and dialogic (two-way), across public services (healthcare, legal, social, education), often with vulnerable service users and power asymmetries — distinct from conference/business interpreting.

## On-assignment business ethics (directly relevant to managing contractors)
- An interpreter "should not seek new business opportunities for his or her own benefit while on assignment." Worked examples: an interpreter with his own business cards never hands them out while working for a language company; a new opportunity offered mid-assignment is accepted only with the formal approval of the contracting provider.
- Implication for Tatkowski: our interpreter-contractor terms must explicitly prohibit soliciting or accepting direct work from our clients met on our assignments (non-circumvention). This is an industry-recognised ethical norm, not just our commercial preference — so it's enforceable and defensible.

## Standards landscape referenced
- ASTM International "Standard Practice for Language Interpreting" (2015); AUSIT Code of Ethics (Australia); NCIHC (US healthcare); the broad move toward tested competency for anyone interpreting, including bilingual staff.

## Professional development (CPD) framing
- The textbook closes on continuing education: "Support the profession: support yourself." Small, consistent learning tasks compound; a documented professional-development plan is encouraged.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **Non-circumvention clause is an industry norm.** Bake "no soliciting/accepting direct work from clients encountered on Tatkowski assignments without written approval" into every interpreter-contractor agreement — and know it's backed by the profession's own ethics, not just our commercial interest. (Reinforces the 5-day rule and "lock interpreter before sharing contact".)
2. **Use the "professional-level qualification" test in our marketing.** Position Tatkowski as supplying only tested, professionally-qualified interpreters — directly contrasting with the "any bilingual person / family member / volunteer" reality the book documents. This is a concrete quality differentiator for B2B (hospitals, courts).
3. **CPD as a quality signal.** A lightweight, documented CPD expectation for our interpreter pool (logged training, terminology upkeep) is cheap and becomes evidence in ISO 17100/18841 work and B2B proposals.
4. **Speak the buyer's taxonomy.** When pitching public-service buyers, use their vocabulary (ISP, community interpreting, language access) — it signals we understand their world.
