# How to be a Successful Freelance Translator (Robert Gebhardt) — Notes
*Practical operations, scaling solo→agency, payment, AIDA. Source read: full text.*

## Prospecting volume (hard numbers)
- 10–20 cold prospects/week minimum at the start.
- <10% response rate is normal.
- Most early leads are agencies, not direct clients.
- Marketing never stops; it just shrinks as a % of time.

## AIDA framework (apply to every pitch)
- **A**ttention — get noticed.
- **I**nterest — lead with benefits/advantages, not features.
- **D**esire — build an emotional/likeability connection; limited-time offers help.
- **A**ction — make the next step obvious (one clear button, not ten links).

## The "So What" test (CRITICAL copy filter)
Re-read every marketing claim and ask "So what?":
- BAD: "I translate 4,000 words/day" / "20 years' experience" / "I use CAT tools" — so what?
- GOOD: "I'll make your website stand out in a crowded market" / "I keep your NY–HK correspondence flowing" / "a competitive edge with your Spanish-speaking customers."
- Every line must state a client BENEFIT, not your attribute.

## Pricing
- Per source word = best (transparent, no surprises).
- Per project = next best (once you can estimate reliably).
- Per hour = worst (race to the bottom, earnings ceiling, sells time not value).
- Context figures: 50–65 characters/line incl. spaces; 1,500–1,800 characters/page; source/target variance up to 30% in Western languages, more in Chinese. Always quote in "source words".

## Outreach templates
- Keep 3–4 templates by specialty (legal, business, general). Personalise: client name (or "Hello"); the job type in their language; your most relevant past project; your rate.
- Don't rewrite from scratch each time (burnout) and don't paste identically (reads as lazy).

## Rush jobs = warning sign
- "Urgent" correlates with bad clients: someone upstream is disorganised; they'll later invent "mistakes" to demand a discount, pay late, and pressure you for chaos they created.
- Decline by default unless it's an existing trusted client; if you accept, charge a premium.

## Tracking spreadsheet (Gebhardt's actual system)
Log per job: job number (chronological); client name; ALL client contacts (emails, phone, address, IM/Skype); job name; word count; rate/word; total due; payment-due date. Invoice weekly, not monthly (terms start from invoice date).

## Invoice essentials
- Big visible TOTAL DUE box in a different colour; Net 7 or "payable on receipt" as default; explicit due date (not just "Net 30"); client PO reference; a marketing tagline in spare invoice space.

## Payment-collection escalation
1. On due date: polite email.
2. Day 2 past due: set a reminder.
3. Day 7: second email; every-other-day reminders.
4. Day 10: email ALL company addresses (info@, contact@) so colleagues/boss see the lateness.
5. Day 14: phone ("calling on behalf of [company]" implies a bigger org).
6. Last resort: an in-person visit by you or a local contact (very effective in-region).
7. Write the public Blue Board review AFTER payment lands.
- Tooling: Boomerang (Gmail) auto-resends if no reply — strong for invoice chasing.
- Escrow: always use it when offered (Upwork etc.) — funds upfront, released on delivery; the cut buys peace of mind.

## Red flags for new clients
Free email only (Gmail/Yahoo, no company domain); no phone; no physical address; asking you to mail personal info; asking YOU to pay a "membership" fee (run).

## Pareto / 80–20 ritual
Common patterns: 80% profit from 20% of clients; 80% of complaints from 20%; 80% of profit from 20% of time. Action: fire the bottom 20% or reset their terms (payment, change limits, no endless chats).

## Scaling: solo → agency
1. **Incorporate** — cheap, fast; signals seriousness; tax + limited-liability benefits; "I have a company" beats "I'm a freelancer." (Tatkowski ✓ — CRO 803790.)
2. **Find a translator** — hire someone like you-when-starting (junior, capable, hungry) on the same platforms; pay less than you charge (justified by your experience, client, PM, reputation); test by checking their first jobs yourself; recruit more than one (cover holidays/sickness).
3. **Find an editor** — similar profile, more grammatically precise; test by editing your trusted translator's work; keeps the translator honest.
4. **Margin math** — if client pays €0.10/word and the translator takes €0.06, the editor must be costed low (e.g. €0.02 for the shorter task) to leave you a real margin (~20%). A 60/40 translator/editor split that consumes the whole client price leaves €0 — broken.

## "Should I tell clients I'm an agency?"
- Yes — be forthcoming. Most don't care; if one does, do that job yourself. Avoids a later "we/I" credibility mismatch; cleaner on liability; less stressful. (Tatkowski IS an agency — own the "we"/"our team" voice.)

## Subcontractor contracts
- Not strictly necessary for legal weight: a written email confirming word count, deadline, fee, and format is usually enough; platforms like Upwork add a legal layer. (Tatkowski: formalise David's arrangement; per-job engagement notes for translator contractors.)

## Time management for solo operators
- Lists/labels/folders so everything's retrievable in seconds; plan tomorrow the night before (especially the first/hardest task); don't open email first thing; cap at 3–4 important tasks/day; document processes for later delegation; use site blockers.
- Recommended reading, in order: *Getting Things Done* (Allen), *The 4-Hour Workweek* (Ferriss), *Work the System* (Carpenter), *The Checklist Manifesto* (Gawande).

## Translator vs editor conflict (watch on our team)
- Editor's incentive is to find changes to justify the fee → risk of over-correction. Defuse by having editors annotate WITH rationale (not bare track-changes) and translators respond constructively, so both look good rather than fighting.
