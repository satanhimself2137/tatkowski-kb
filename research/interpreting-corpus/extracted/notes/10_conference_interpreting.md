*Large (1.8MB) interpreter-TRAINING curriculum + Trainer's Guide. Business/cashflow value is genuinely thin — it teaches how to train conference interpreters, not how to run an agency. Source read: professional-practice and market-organisation sections sampled and verified.*

## Honest assessment
This is a pedagogy bible (AIIC-aligned course design, booth skills, relay, exams). It deliberately points to **AIIC** for actual fee structures and working-conditions standards rather than stating rates itself. The commercially-useful content is limited to a few structural facts about how the conference market works.

## What's genuinely usable for Tatkowski

### AIIC is the authority for conference-interpreting standards
- The book builds on AIIC (Association internationale des interprètes de conférence) best practices throughout. For conference work, AIIC defines the recognised norms on team strength, working conditions, and professional conduct. → If/when Tatkowski quotes conference interpreting, AIIC norms are the reference to cite for credibility and to justify team/working-condition terms.

### Conference interpreting requires TEAMS (booth economics)
- Simultaneous interpreting (SI) is done in booths and structurally relies on teamwork; interpreters work in pairs/teams and rotate, and multilingual events use **relay** through a "pivot" interpreter.
- Commercial implication: a conference quote is inherently a multi-interpreter quote (booths × interpreters per booth + equipment), never a single-person day rate. This is the professional standard, which protects margin and quality.

### "Working conditions" are a professional entitlement, not a luxury
- The text stresses interpreters "should aim to secure essential working conditions" (preparation time, document access, presence, proper equipment) even when optimal comfort isn't guaranteed.
- Commercial implication: build preparation time, advance documents, and equipment requirements into conference T&Cs as standard line items — framed as quality/accuracy requirements, not add-ons.

### Market-readiness / "the last mile"
- Modern interpreters must handle sub-optimal real-world conditions (speed, technicality, accents, recited text, remote/"oral translation" mode at the UN and increasingly elsewhere).
- Commercial implication: when sourcing conference contractors, screen for real-world resilience, not just exam pedigree.

### Industry structure note
- In the Japanese system, conference interpreters are mostly trained inside the training departments of a few large private-sector translation agencies — an example of the agency-as-talent-pipeline model (agency trains → agency staffs). Long-horizon relevance for Tatkowski Systems if we ever build an in-house training pipeline.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **Conference interpreting is a team product — quote it that way.** Never price a conference as a single interpreter. Price booths × interpreters + equipment + prep time, citing AIIC norms. This is both the quality standard and a margin protector. (Consistent with Mikkelson's team-interpreting point for long legal sessions.)
2. **Cite AIIC for conference credibility.** For any conference-interpreting B2B proposal, reference AIIC working-condition and team-strength standards — it signals we operate at professional norms, not bargain-basement.
3. **Bake working conditions into conference T&Cs.** Advance documents, preparation time, and proper equipment as standard requirements, framed as accuracy safeguards.
4. **Conference interpreting is NOT our near-term lead product.** Given the team/equipment overhead and AIIC-grade talent requirements, it's a higher-complexity, higher-cost offering. Keep B2B liaison/court/medical interpreting as Priority 1 (per strategy); treat conference work as opportunistic/high-margin, sourced carefully, not a core push.
5. **Lowest commercial yield of the corpus.** Logged for completeness; no pricing playbook here — AIIC is the reference if we pursue conference work seriously.
