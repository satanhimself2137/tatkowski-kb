# The Rise of Conference Interpreting in China (Irene A. Zhang & Riccardo Moratto, eds., 2024) — Notes
*Historical/biographical insider account of how conference interpreting developed in China (UN/EU cooperation, training programmes, practitioner memoirs). Geographic case study — almost no transferable pricing/cashflow content for an Irish agency. Source read: market/economics passages located and verified.*

## Honest assessment
This is an edited memoir collection tracing the profession's growth in China — valuable as history, not as an operations manual. Most of it (UN Chinese booth history, Beijing training programmes, DG SCIC cooperation, personal career arcs) has no bearing on Tatkowski's day-to-day. Two passages carry a genuinely transferable lesson.

## The one durable lesson: scarcity → premium pricing ("the halo")
- Verbatim: "With a daily interpreting fee of around RMB 6000 while a white-collar office worker made RMB 2000 a month, there was a halo over the head of a conference interpreter."
- A single day's interpreting paid 3× a month's white-collar salary. That premium existed because qualified conference interpreters were scarce and demand (China opening up, international engagement) was surging.
- The predictable second-order effect, also in the text: the premium drew a rush of low-quality entrants and people "wanting to make a quick buck offering interpreter training" — i.e. scarcity pricing attracts dilution, which is exactly why entry control + quality signalling matter (ties to Tseng stage 4 in the PSI notes).

## Supply shortage persists even in a mature segment
- A current UN-side practitioner notes "there is currently a serious shortage of freelance interpreters on the market," to the point it constrains how recruitment evaluations can even be applied.
- Same structural signal as Mikkelson's court-interpreter shortage: demand for qualified interpreters routinely outstrips supply, especially at the top of the quality distribution.

## Structural note
- China's elite interpreters were built through dedicated, selective training programmes (often agency- or institution-run) with stringent mock-conference practice and senior mentorship — reinforcing the agency-as-talent-pipeline pattern also seen in the Japanese model (Conference Interpreting notes).

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **Scarcity is priced — position for it.** When Tatkowski supplies genuinely scarce capability (a qualified court/medical interpreter in a less-common language, on short notice, in-market across IE/UK/ES/PT), that scarcity justifies premium pricing. Don't discount scarce, high-stakes work toward commodity rates — the "halo" is real and the buyer expects to pay for reliability.
2. **Scarcity attracts dilution — defend with quality signals.** The same premium that rewards us attracts low-quality entrants. Our moat is verifiable quality: ATII/IQA qualifications, the 8 Canons, ISO 17100, Statement of Truth/Accuracy. Lean on these in B2B proposals precisely because the market is noisy with cheap entrants.
3. **The interpreter-supply shortage is structural and global.** Reinforces sourcing as a strategic priority (David's PT/ES interpreter sourcing, ATII directory in IE). Owning a reliable, vetted interpreter roster IS the scarce asset — invest in recruiting and retaining it.
4. **Lowest transferable yield of the corpus** (with Conference Interpreting). Logged for completeness; the durable takeaway is the scarcity-pricing/quality-defence dynamic, which the practitioner-facing books (Jenner, McKay) already operationalise.
