# The Entrepreneurial Linguist (Judy & Dagmar Jenner) — Notes
*Focus: business model, pricing, direct clients, cashflow. Source read: full text, chs 1–7.*

## Ch 1 — The New Mindset
- Stop thinking "freelancer", start thinking "business owner". US small-business context: 99.7% of employers are small businesses; ~78% have no employees (one-person). The point: a solo language professional IS a real business and should behave like one.
- Selling proposition vs large agencies: direct contact with the decision-maker, faster decisions, lower overhead → you can price competitively AND keep margin.
- Do NOT fake "we" plural to look bigger — misleading and it backfires. Be transparent about who you are.
- Urgent-project case study: accept, but charge a 24-hour turnaround premium. "Few professionals will expect you to work through the night for the same price you would during the day."
- Large-project deposit case study (50,000+ words): structure as **30% upfront / 30% midpoint / 40% on delivery**, signed quote required. Waive only on strong referral.

## Ch 2 — Organization & Accounting
- Separate business bank account + business credit card from day one.
- Decide CPA vs software based on value-add (complex deductions → CPA).
- Log receipts daily/weekly (10 min/day beats a monthly pile-up).
- Always know your AR (accounts receivable) and AP (accounts payable).
- The 30/30/40 structure is the operating default for large jobs.

## Ch 4 — Marketing (CASHFLOW-CRITICAL)

### Repeat customers are the cheapest revenue
- Far cheaper to keep a client than to win a new one.
- Follow up within days of delivery — most contractors never follow up, so it stands out.
- Deliver in batches if the client is under time pressure.
- Monthly check-in with favourite clients about their upcoming pipeline.
- Go the extra mile: deliver early, refer out work you can't do, throw in a quick free analysis.

### Pricing tactics for repeat/ongoing work
- Flat fee for ongoing updates (e.g. website refreshes) so the client can budget and your income smooths.
- Sell time blocks (e.g. "5 hours of translation services") so micro-jobs are frictionless.
- A custom rate for a verified high-volume repeat client can be worth it — frame it as exclusive ("one of only two clients on this rate").
- Handwritten note + a small discount sticky-note on a printed invoice doubles as a miniature ad.

### Why customers leave (control what you can)
1. Their internal contact leaves — UNCONTROLLABLE → diversify your contacts inside each client.
2. They go out of business — UNCONTROLLABLE → diversify your portfolio.
3. They no longer need the service — UNCONTROLLABLE.
4. They're unhappy — CONTROLLABLE → have an honest conversation, fix it.
5. They chose another vendor — PARTLY CONTROLLABLE.
- Pareto: ~80% of revenue from ~20% of clients. Identify and fire bad clients (slow payers, hagglers, abusive, chronic-rush).

### Brand & contact cadence
- Logo on everything: cards, letterhead, quotes, invoices, address labels, website.
- Keep up meaningful contact (even a "payment received, thank you" note).
- Cheap HTML newsletter (Constant Contact-type) or barter with an HTML-savvy friend.

### Customer concierge habits
- Take notes during calls; repeat back ("To recap, you'd like…").
- Send a recap email after every important call.
- Track personal context (surgery, busy season, preferred channel/hours).
- Adapt to the client's tools and formats, not yours.

### Competitive advantage
- "Fast / reliable / high quality" are table stakes, not differentiators.
- Real differentiators: subject-matter PhD, bicultural (not just bilingual), prior industry career, security clearance, narrow specialism. Brainstorm with a colleague to find yours.

### Cold calling vs introductions
- Cold calling converts ~2% (cites Joanne Black) — poor ROI.
- Use the introduction model ("presentar"): identify the target → find a mutual connection → formally ask for the intro (it's an honour, not a right) → take both to lunch (you pay) → CC the introducer on the first email → always thank the introducer regardless of outcome.

### Specialization rules
- Know your NON-specializations too — refuse and refer that work out (ethical + protects quality).
- "Warren Buffett rule": if you don't understand the field, don't take it.
- Best specialism growth is organic, out of existing clients' adjacent needs.

### Don'ts and Dos
- Don't chase people who already said no; don't pitch clients who already have a good provider.
- Do follow up with "not now, later" prospects exactly when they asked.
- Do read between the lines of polite refusals.

### Cheap acquisition channels
- Local/industry press: pitch a story angle; use free press-release sites; keep releases to 3–6 short paragraphs with a strong headline.
- ~30 minutes every few months is enough to maintain this.

### Marketing materials
- NO résumés to direct clients — they're buying a service, not hiring an employee.
- Communicate five things: what you can do for THEM; relevant industry-targeted experience; why they can trust you; your academic background; your specific competitive advantage.
- PDF everything you send (locks layout, prevents edits). Good paper. Professional photo. Refresh materials a few times a year. Avoid free business cards that advertise the printer on the back.

### Website
- A cheap DIY site is fine; sections: About, Contact, Background, Services, Rates, Clients, Testimonials, Blog. Memorable, short URL. A site only helps if people can find it.

## Ch 5 — Business Development (DIRECT-CLIENT ACQUISITION)
- Prerequisites: finish your plan/strategy/marketing first; know your competitive advantage; pick an industry focus; identify its events, trade groups, and publications.
- "Yes you can" — every excuse ("too busy", "shy", "no ideas") is workable.
- Go where the CLIENTS are (industry events), not where translators are.
- Quality of contacts beats a stack of business cards.
- Be a solution provider: research the company, identify a language problem they may not know they have, propose specific value — never "let me know if you need anything".

### Direct-client acquisition case study (THE PATTERN TO COPY)
1. Pick 3–5 target companies in your priority sector.
2. Read their industry press; understand their pain.
3. Find the right contact at each (LinkedIn etc.).
4. Find a mutual connection.
5. Ask for the intro without being pushy; let the introducer use their own words.
6. Pre-arrange a meeting at their convenience (e.g. during a trade show they're attending).
7. Show up prepared; know their company.
8. Thank both the contact and the introducer.
9. Be patient — in the example, the first project landed ~5 months after the meeting.

### Pro bono & pre-qualified contacts
- Pro bono opens doors (free school career day → the school's paid translation work).
- Pre-qualified leads via mutual connections convert far better than cold.

### Trade-show strategy
- Pre-research ~20 target companies from the exhibitor list; customise materials per company; pre-schedule meetings; volunteer at the event for free admission; bring small problem-solver touches; follow up within a week (people are buried the first week back).

### Resource allocation
- Every event has an opportunity cost (hours = lost billable time or rest). Say no to events that don't fit the strategy. Trial and error is expected.

## Ch 6 — Pricing (MOST CRITICAL CHAPTER)
- Don't compete on price — someone always undercuts; the race to the bottom commoditises you.
- Avocado principle: don't be the $0.80 commodity avocado; be the premium organic one (higher demand, less competition, better price).
- Seller sets the price, buyer accepts or declines: "Thank you for telling me your rate. My rate is X." Professional services aren't haggled like a market stall. BMW principle: quality justifies the price.
- When price pressure arrives, flex soft costs, not the base rate — the **5 alternatives to discounting**:
  1. Discount the rush fee.
  2. Discount the PDF/handling surcharge.
  3. Throw in a bonus (early delivery, source review).
  4. Apply a non-profit rate (last resort, genuine non-profits only).
  5. Honour a recently expired promo.
- Adjust prices for inflation YEARLY (US 3–5%; more in less stable currencies). Inform, don't ask — communicate via an asterisk on the rate sheet, an email, or a note on the next invoice. It's B2B; clients do the same.
- Never advertise prices as "negotiable". Keep a rate sheet. Prices are firm.

## Ch 7 — Negotiating
- Know your bottom line (price floor + firm parameters like the delivery date).
- Avoid price negotiation where you can; educate clients that professional services aren't haggled.
- Referenced piece: "The Vendor Client Relationship in the Real World" (the satirical video where a customer haggles a coffee shop the way clients haggle freelancers).

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **30/30/40 on large jobs.** Any B2B job ≥3 weeks / ≥~€2k: 30% on signed quote, 30% midpoint, 40% on delivery. Waive upfront only on strong referral.
2. **The 5 alternatives to discounting.** Train everyone to flex rush/PDF/bonus/non-profit/expired-promo before ever touching the per-page base rate.
3. **Competitive-advantage filter.** Stop selling "fast/reliable/quality". Sell: court-accepted certified translation, ATII/IQA-qualified interpreters, 4-market coverage under one invoice, B2B invoicing with 30-day terms.
4. **Kill cold calling; build the intro engine.** Target named contacts, find mutual connections on LinkedIn, ask for warm intros, CC the introducer, always thank them.
5. **Repeat-customer engine.** Follow up within days; monthly pipeline check-ins with top clients; exclusive flat rates for high-volume repeats; handwritten thank-you + sticky discount on invoices.
6. **Annual inflation raise.** 3–5% every year across the board; inform via invoice note. Don't silently absorb inflation.
7. **Fire the bottom 20%.** Run the Pareto cut: slow payers, hagglers, missed-deadline clients, abusive ones — free the capacity for better clients.
