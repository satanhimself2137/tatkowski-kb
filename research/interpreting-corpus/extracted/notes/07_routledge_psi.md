# The Routledge Handbook of Public Service Interpreting (ed. Laura Gavioli, 2022) — Notes
*PSI market structure, procurement, the outsourcing crisis, professionalisation. Source read: professionalisation + outsourcing passages verified against text.*

## Market intelligence (verbatim-grounded)

### The outsourcing / de-professionalisation crisis (core opportunity)
- The text is explicit: governments "outsource translation and interpreting services to private companies: this is, however, not ideal," and "outsourced management by a private company has not proved ideal, but policymakers have shown little sensitivity."
- Gentile (2017): privatisation and outsourcing of PSI have **hindered professionalisation and damaged public perception**, and are "destroying systems where service providers were once encouraged to use registered and state-certified interpreters (for instance, in the United Kingdom and the Netherlands)."
- Post-2008 cuts "transformed PSI into a commodity … creating a situation in which the winner is a cheap service rather than a quality one."
- COVID "boosted the need for interpreting services" while setting up a new economic squeeze.
- PSI is still characterised as a "semi-profession" — has some professional traits but lacks full autonomy.

### Tseng's (1992) four-stage professionalisation model (verbatim)
1. Market disorder + fierce competition among practitioners, with a complete lack of social recognition.
2. Development of consensus about practitioners' aspirations.
3. Creation of professional associations and codes of conduct → higher recognition and prestige.
4. Adherence to the code of ethics + control of entry → consolidates the profession.
- (Mikkelson 1996 notes progressing 1→4 isn't guaranteed.)
- Tatkowski reading of our markets: IE ~stage 2–3 (ATII active, no statutory regulation); UK ~stage 3 but eroded by outsourcing; ES ~stage 2 for PSI (though traducción jurada is a mature, stage-4-like sworn system); PT ~stage 2 and developing.

### Demand drivers
- EU Directive 2010/64/EU (interpreting/translation in criminal proceedings); rising migration/asylum; healthcare and court access laws; COVID-driven growth in remote interpreting.

### Quality-crisis drivers (our wedge)
- 2008 budget cuts → cheapest-provider selection; outsourcing to private companies → quality drops; lower qualification bars → unqualified interpreters → errors; low pay → talent leaves → vicious cycle.

### Procurement realities
- Public-sector work runs on framework agreements and multi-year contracts; lowest-price-wins is slowly giving way to quality-based criteria; ISO standards increasingly feature as procurement requirements; pre-qualification questionnaires filter on certifications, capacity, references; professional-indemnity insurance is commonly required.

### Relevant organisations
- EULITA (European Legal Interpreters and Translators Association); ENPSI; NRPSI (UK National Register of Public Service Interpreters); AIIC; ATII (IE, already in our network).

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **Quality-first positioning is real and evidence-backed.** There is documented dissatisfaction with low-cost outsourced PSI. Position Tatkowski credibly on ATII/IQA-qualified interpreters, ISO 17100 in pursuit, Statement of Truth/Accuracy on every translation, and direct accountability (no double-middleman).
2. **B2B-first interpreting is the right strategy.** B2B buyers (hospitals, law firms, courts) value quality over price and give stable, repeat work; B2C is more price-sensitive and lower-volume.
3. **ISO 17100 / 18841 is a procurement moat.** These standards are turning into requirements for public/B2B contracts — our Q3 2026 ISO 17100 pursuit is correctly prioritised. (18841 for interpreting is the natural follow-on.)
4. **EU Directive 2010/64 is an ongoing tailwind**, unevenly implemented across the EU; ES/PT are still professionalising, so our presence there is well-timed.
5. **Insurance is a gating requirement.** For B2B/public tenders, professional-indemnity cover (commonly €1–5M) is standard. Confirm our level, raise if needed before pursuing tenders, and cite it as evidence in proposals.
