## The business of interpreting (verbatim-grounded)
- Most interpreter training ignores the business side: marketing, client relations, letters of agreement, budgeting, overhead, billing, payment terms, taxation. Freelancers must handle all of it and should write a business plan before starting.
- To launch as a court interpreter: get listed in any jurisdictional register, obtain the required credential, ask the local judiciary how interpreters are hired. Build a website/social presence; distribute business cards to legal professionals.
- First impression matters: punctuality, proper business attire, professional conduct.
- **"Word of mouth is far more valuable than any other marketing tool … because lawyers are a tight-knit group and they often exchange information about interpreters."** ← primary acquisition channel.
- The book explicitly cross-references ATA's "The Entrepreneurial Linguist" column and *How to Succeed as a Freelance Translator* (so this corpus is internally consistent — these books cite each other).

## The Code of Ethics — the 8 Canons (verbatim, adopt as our contractor standard)
1. **Accuracy** — faithful rendering; no addition/omission/explanation/paraphrase; preserve register, style, tone, hedges, false starts; seek clarification rather than guess; correct errors on the record.
2. **Impartiality and conflicts of interest** — remain neutral, maintain the appearance of neutrality, avoid unnecessary contact with parties, disclose any real/potential conflict immediately.
3. **Confidentiality** — no disclosure of privileged/confidential information without authorisation.
4. **Limitations of practice** — interpret/translate only; never give legal advice or anything construable as practising law.
5. **Protocol and demeanor** — conduct per court protocol; be unobtrusive; speak in the same grammatical person as the speaker; flag when speaking for yourself.
6. **Maintenance and improvement of skills and knowledge** — keep skills current.
7. **Accurate representation of credentials** — don't overstate certifications/experience.
8. **Impediments to compliance** — proactively flag anything preventing compliance: fatigue, inability to hear, inadequate specialised terminology.

## Remote interpreting (growing segment, verified)
- Defined (Braun) as using communication technology to access an interpreter elsewhere.
- Earliest remote interpreting was by telephone (OPI), launched in Australia in the 1970s; still widely used. Video remote interpreting (VRI) began replacing telephone as the predominant mode.
- The main driver institutions cite is cost-saving.
- Research (AVIDICUS project and others) finds remote interpreting more error-prone and harder for turn-taking than face-to-face; problems compound as proceedings run long.
- Best-practice guidance noted in the text: remote interpreting needs special training; technical support should be available immediately; participants should identify themselves each time they speak; and there's an explicit recommendation to use a break/relief or extra care for any event likely to last **more than 30 minutes**.

## Market signals for Tatkowski
- Court-interpreter shortage is a recurring industry theme (the book cites the broader literature on access-to-justice and interpreter scarcity, especially for less-common languages). Shortage → court delays → costs for the system and the parties.
- EU Directive 2010/64/EU (right to interpreting/translation in criminal proceedings) recurs throughout as the regulatory backbone in Europe.
- Lawyers refer to other lawyers — embedding with a few firms seeds wider referral.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **Adopt the 8 Canons explicitly** in interpreter-contractor engagement terms. A named ethical standard is both a quality differentiator and liability protection — quote it in B2B proposals.
2. **Lawyer word-of-mouth is the channel.** Do 1–2 excellent jobs for top-tier IE firms, then explicitly ask for referrals; target partners directly (the Jenner intro/lunch model fits perfectly).
3. **Build a VRI offering at a premium** (≈15–20% over standard) for out-of-hours court emergencies, rural courts lacking in-language interpreters, mental-health tribunals, and asylum proceedings — but market it as quality-controlled VRI, distinct from cheap OPI.
4. **Team interpreting for long sessions.** For B2B trials/long sessions, quote two interpreters as the professional standard for anything sustained (the 30-minute relief guidance and fatigue Canon back this up) — present it as standard practice, not upselling.
5. **EU Directive 2010/64 is a tailwind**, especially in ES/PT where court-interpreting professionalisation is still catching up — our presence there is well-timed.
