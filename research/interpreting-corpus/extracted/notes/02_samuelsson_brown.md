# A Practical Guide for Translators (Geoffrey Samuelsson-Brown) — Notes
*Agency operations, quality control, getting paid, client education. Source read: full text, chs 3, 4, 8, 10.*

## Ch 3 — The client's viewpoint

### Client misconceptions to correct (with the counter-script)
1. "The translator works alone and needs no input." → Dialogue is essential; push back on poorly written or ambiguous source.
2. "5,000 words overnight for a tiny fee." → This is skilled professional labour, not a commodity typing job.
3. "Just tidy up my own draft." → Often costs as much as a fresh translation; offer a proper translation instead.
4. "The computer does the work now, so charge less." → The client pays for knowledge and judgement, not keystrokes.
5. "Just give me a rough translation." → "We don't produce rough translations. We can give you a short abstract (≤300 words) for fact-finding, or a properly checked translation."

### The 5 Quality Gaps (audit each)
- Gap 1: what the client EXPECTS ≠ what the PM thinks they want.
- Gap 2: the PM's understanding ≠ the written spec given to the translator.
- Gap 3: the spec ≠ what's actually delivered.
- Gap 4: the advertising promise ≠ the actual delivery.
- Gap 5: the client's prior expectations ≠ the delivered experience.
- Mitigation: a written spec for every job; repeat back; confirm in writing.

### Provider-type positioning
A freelance / small agency / medium non-accredited / ISO-accredited grid on language range, in-house translators, independent checking, project management, and capacity for major projects. Tatkowski currently sits between "medium non-accredited" and "ISO-accredited" — ISO 17100 is the next moat.

## Ch 4 — Running a translation business

### Counting words
- UK convention: per 1,000 source words. Agree the counting method up front.
- Number blocks: 3 blocks of figures counted as 1 word for charging.
- Target text can run ±30% vs source (Romance ↔ English).
- ~300 words per A4 page as a rough estimate (range 50–1,000).

### Quotations
- NEVER quote sight-unseen — always ask for sample pages.
- Use a structured costing table; state a quote validity window (1 or 3 months); add VAT where applicable; build a contingency INTO the rate rather than itemising "miscellaneous".

### Overtime / rush
- Evening work +50%; weekend work +100%. The "frantic Friday afternoon for Monday" job is a decline-by-default.

### Cashflow — the brutal truth
- "Profitable, expanding businesses can go bankrupt because they run out of cash."
- Sales = vanity, profit = sanity, cash in the bank = reality.
- Rough model: ~60% of invoices paid the month after invoicing, ~40% the month after that.
- Set tax aside monthly into a separate interest-bearing account.
- No single client should exceed ~25% of revenue.

### Late-payment law
- UK: Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998 — statutory interest, 30-day default if no terms agreed. (EU equivalent: Directive 2011/7/EU, transposed in IE/ES/PT.)

## Ch 8 — Quality control & accountability

### 5 categories of translation by purpose (each needs different QC)
1. Information extraction — facts/figures accuracy; abstract style is fine.
2. Abstracting — condensing a long source; editorial skill.
3. Information (full translation) — accurate, need not be literary.
4. For publication — high QC; you may be liable for downstream printing costs if you err.
5. Legal / notarised / certified — HIGHEST QC; footnote legal concepts with no target equivalent; Statement of Truth/Accuracy.

### Production-rate benchmarks
- Staff translator ~35,000 words/month including checking others' work.
- ~1,500 source words/day of new translation is sustainable.
- Dictation can hit ~2,200 words/hr but is unsustainable and needs more checking.
- Checking someone else's work ~1,500 words/hr (≈4 A4 pages); proofing your own is slower.
- Planning rule for Tatkowski: 1,500 words/day/translator for new work, then add a ~30% buffer for QC + formatting.

### Pre-acceptance questions (intake checklist)
Subject and your confidence in it; sample pages; volume + deadline; what the translation is FOR; reference material/past translations; who answers queries; required format/presentation; whether the client's side will review and feed back comments; house style.

### Be honest with the client
- Decline unrealistic deadlines: "We would rather turn down work than produce a poor-quality translation that could damage our reputation."
- If you must take a tight job, mark the running header "UNCHECKED DRAFT", check it the next morning, and flag every change.

## Ch 10 — When things go wrong (CASHFLOW)

### Missing deadlines
- Call AHEAD of the deadline, never go silent; put concerns in writing as soon as a job arrives late or looks at risk.

### Slow-pay / insolvent clients
- Agree terms in writing up front and make them bilateral: "We don't accept your stated terms; ours are 30 days net by mutual agreement."
- Set a credit limit per new client.
- For >3-week jobs, stage the payments (30/30/40 — matches Jenner).
- Get a written purchase order from direct/B2B clients; check references on new ones.

### The 9 excuses for late payment (pattern library)
1. "The cheque is in the post."
2. "It arrived after the cheque run — next month."
3. "The person who signs is on holiday/sick/away."
4. "We need a statement, not just an invoice."
5. "I've signed it; it's on the boss's desk."
6. "It's been passed to accounts" (and accounts say they never received it).
7. "Lost in the post — please resend" (then the cycle restarts).
8. "X no longer works here."
9. "Accounts are in another building."
- Hear two of these → escalate; don't wait for a third.

### Recovery action
- County/small-claims court (UK <£5k) needs no solicitor; the mere threat often triggers payment because it affects credit rating.
- A professional body (ITI) can advise on tardy agencies; file as a creditor if a client becomes insolvent.

### Indemnity insurance
- Essential for legal/publication work; a sole proprietor's personal assets are exposed to a successful judgment.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **Client-education scripts.** Pre-write polished responses to the five classic misconceptions (tidy-up, overnight-cheap, computer-does-it, rough-translation, no-input) for inbound enquiries.
2. **The 5 QC Gaps audit** in every B2B onboarding: confirm expectations in writing → spec to translator → check delivered-vs-spec → audit our marketing claims → post-delivery experience check.
3. **Translation-purpose intake.** Classify every quote as information-extraction / abstract / information / publication / legal-certified — drives QC level and price.
4. **Cashflow discipline.** No client >25% of revenue; per-client credit limits; 30/30/40 on >3-week jobs; written PO from every B2B; documented, mutually-agreed 30-day terms; 60/40 collection assumption; tax set aside monthly.
5. **Capacity model.** 1,500 source words/day/translator new work, 1,500 words/hr checking, +30% buffer. Sell within capacity; decline rather than fail.
