# How to Succeed as a Freelance Translator (Corinne McKay) — Notes
*Rates, contracts, payment, direct clients, growth. Source read: marketing chapters + ch 10 (rates/contracts/terms) in full.*

## Marketing chapters — key numbers
- ~300–500 contacts in year one to build toward full-time.
- ~1% response rate on cold outreach — expect ~99 non-replies per yes.
- Marketing stays ~10% of time even when established ("if you're not marketing, you're dying").
- Sustainable throughput ~400–600 words/hour; ~2,000–3,000/day.
- 25–50% of time is non-billable (marketing, admin, billing, downtime).

### Billable-hours pricing model (CRITICAL)
- 40 hrs × 48 wks ≈ 1,920 potential hours/year.
- Minus sick days, holidays, and non-billable time → ~1,068 billable hours.
- Required hourly rate = (target salary + business costs + profit) ÷ billable hours.
- Worked example in the book: ~$55,750 ÷ 1,068 ≈ $52/hr.

### Per-word vs per-hour
- Per-word: client knows the cost up front; rewards your efficiency; lets clients compare like-for-like.
- Per-hour: removes your loss risk but caps earnings.
- Recommendation: per-word for most work, per-hour reserved for editing (where revision time varies wildly).

## Ch 10 — Rates, contracts, terms (HIGH VALUE)

### Rate-sheet structure
1. Standard rate (most jobs).
2. Volume discount (cautious — a big job tied up at a discount is a lost-opportunity risk if they pay late).
3. Rush charge (above standard for 24-hour/weekend).
4. Minimum charge (a floor, ~$20–50/€25–50, to cover admin overhead on tiny jobs).
5. Editing rate (¼–⅓ of the translation rate).
6. TM/repetition discount (optional fuzzy-match tiers).

### When the client asks for a discount — the perfect reply
> "I'm unable to offer a discount, because I'm busy all the time at my regular rates."
- True (and if not, work on being busy); not defensive; not negotiation theatre; price-sensitive clients self-select out.

### Contract clauses to read carefully (RED FLAGS)
1. "We pay you only after the end-client pays us" — you'd absorb the agency's credit risk. Dangerous.
2. "Indemnify us against lawsuits" — you need E&O / professional-indemnity insurance.
3. "Non-compete with our clients" — reasonable only where there's genuine client overlap.
4. "No subcontracting" — read before signing; comply if you sign.
5. Confidentiality / NDAs — read carefully, especially for financial/legal work.
6. Credit/background check / bonding — know exactly what you're agreeing to share.

### Your own Terms of Service — clauses to ADD
1. No claims accepted after X days from invoice (2–4 weeks).
2. Claims limited to the invoice amount (cap downstream liability).
3. The client's TOS isn't in force until you approve it in writing (stops post-hoc terms).
4. Your agreement is with the client only, not their end-client.
5. You retain copyright until the invoice is paid in full (leverage for non-payment).
6. Cancellation: you're paid for work completed up to the cancellation point.
7. You must be given the chance to correct before any payment reduction.

### Pre-acceptance questions (intake)
Subject; deadline + timezone (specify, not "end of day"); word/page count; format (PDF/Word/HTML/scan/handwriting); TM required and any discount; base rate; special instructions (glossary, formatting).

### Client due diligence before accepting
Full business name, website, physical address, phone; Whois on the domain; ProZ Blue Board (paid membership); Payment Practices (paymentpractices.net — ~$20/yr, worth it); Experian credit check for large agencies; references from other translators.

### Non-paying-client escalation ladder
1. First/second/final notice — final one by certified mail with return receipt.
2. Post to Payment Practices (factual only — permitted).
3. Tell the client you're posting (often triggers payment).
4. Hire a collection agency (worth it for a large invoice).
5. Contact the end-client (last resort — usually contractually forbidden).
6. Small-claims court.
7. Write it off and treat non-payment as a cost of doing business.

### Payment terms by region
- US: Net 30. Europe: Net 60, sometimes Net 60 EOM (end-of-month). Plan cashflow for the slow-pay regions.

### International payment methods
- Wire transfer (secure but fees + FX loss); foreign-currency cheque (slow); overseas bank account (volume only, accounting overhead); PayPal (cheap on small amounts); Wise/TransferWise (much cheaper than banks). For Tatkowski: Revolut Business already covers multi-currency with per-currency IBANs.

### Invoice essentials
- Logical invoice number (year + counter or initials + counter); tax ID; clear "bill to" + payee; PO reference if supplied.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS FOR TATKOWSKI

1. **The discount reply, verbatim.** "I'm unable to offer a discount, because I'm busy all the time at my regular rates." B2B variant: "Our rates reflect operational capacity; we're at full utilisation at our published rates."
2. **The 7 ToS clauses** — add every one to our B2B contract: claims window, claims capped at invoice, our approval required for their TOS, agreement-with-client-only, copyright-until-paid, cancellation-pro-rata, right-to-cure before any deduction.
3. **The intake checklist** — confirm SalesManager captures: subject, deadline+timezone, word/page count, format, TM, base rate, special instructions, translation purpose.
4. **Non-payment process, baked in:** invoice → due day 30 → friendly nudge day 35–40 → second notice day 60 → final registered notice day 75 → collections/small-claims day 90 (IE small claims €2,000; UK £5,000) → factual public posting.
5. **Blended-rate sanity check.** Required rate = (owner draw + overhead + profit) ÷ billable hours. Roughly (€40k + €20k + 20%) ÷ ~1,000 hrs ≈ €72/hr → at ~500 words/hr ≈ €0.14/word ≈ €42/300-word page — broadly consistent with our €29.99–€49.99 page pricing across markets.
