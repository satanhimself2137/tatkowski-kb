## 1. THE PRICING PLAYBOOK

### 1.1 Don't compete on price (Jenner, McKay, China)
Someone always undercuts; the race to the bottom commoditises you. Jenner's avocado principle: be the premium organic one, not the $0.80 commodity. The China memoir shows the flip side — genuine scarcity carried a "halo" (a day's interpreting = 3× a month's office salary) because qualified supply was thin. Lesson: price scarce, high-stakes work at a premium; defend it with quality signals because premiums attract low-quality entrants.

### 1.2 Seller sets the price (Jenner)
When a client states their rate: "Thank you for telling me your rate. My rate is X." Professional services aren't haggled like a market stall. BMW principle — quality justifies price.

### 1.3 The discount reply (McKay) — the single most useful line in the corpus
> "I'm unable to offer a discount, because I'm busy all the time at my regular rates."
B2B variant: "Our rates reflect operational capacity; we're at full utilisation at our published rates." True, non-defensive, ends the negotiation, lets price-shoppers self-select out.

### 1.4 The 5 alternatives to discounting (Jenner) — flex soft costs, never the base rate
1. Discount the rush fee. 2. Discount the PDF/handling surcharge. 3. Throw in a bonus (early delivery, source review). 4. Non-profit rate (genuine non-profits only). 5. Honour a recently expired promo. The per-page/per-word base stays put.

### 1.5 Annual inflation raise (Jenner)
Raise 3–5% every year across the board. Inform, don't ask — asterisk on the rate sheet or a note on the next invoice. B2B clients do it; so do we. Silently absorbing inflation erodes margin every year.

### 1.6 Rate-sheet architecture (McKay)
Standard rate; volume discount (cautiously — a big discounted job tied up is lost-opportunity risk if they pay late); rush charge; minimum charge (admin-overhead floor on tiny jobs); editing rate (¼–⅓ of translation); optional TM/repetition tiers. Tatkowski already has standard/urgent + handwriting (+€5) + postage (+€10); the missing pieces to define are an explicit minimum charge and (if we offer editing) an editing rate.

### 1.7 Per-word > per-project > per-hour (Gebhardt, McKay)
Per source word: transparent, rewards efficiency, like-for-like comparable. Per project: fine once you estimate reliably. Per hour: AVOID — caps earnings, sells time not value (exception: editing, where revision time varies). Page rate is our B2C translation unit; hour is the B2B interpreting unit.

### 1.8 Never quote sight-unseen (Samuelsson-Brown)
Always request sample pages first. ~300 words/A4 page rough estimate; target text ±30% vs source; agree the counting method (source words) up front. Number blocks: 3 = 1 word.

### 1.9 Rush/overtime premiums (Samuelsson-Brown, Jenner, Gebhardt)
Evening +50%, weekend +100% as reference points. "Frantic Friday for Monday" — decline by default. Crucially (Gebhardt): an urgent job from a NEW client is a bad-client signal (upstream disorganisation → late payment, invented "mistakes", change demands). Accept rush only from trusted clients, at a premium.

### 1.10 Interpreting is quoted as a team product where applicable (Mikkelson, Setton & Dawrant)
Long legal sessions and all conference work need ≥2 interpreters (fatigue/relay). Quote booths × interpreters + equipment + prep time, citing NAJIT/AIIC norms — professional standard AND margin protector, not upselling.

## 2. THE CASHFLOW DISCIPLINE

### 2.1 30/30/40 on large jobs (Jenner, Samuelsson-Brown — agree)
Any job ≥3 weeks / ≥~€2k: 30% on signed quote, 30% midpoint, 40% on delivery. Signed quote required; waive the upfront only on a strong referral. Two independent sources converge on staged payment for large work.

### 2.2 No client > 25% of revenue (Samuelsson-Brown)
Above 25% = existential risk if they leave/fail. When any client nears 20%, deliberately diversify in-sector.

### 2.3 Cash is reality (Samuelsson-Brown)
"Sales = vanity, profit = sanity, cash in the bank = reality." Forecast ~60% of invoices paid the month after invoicing, ~40% the month after. Set tax aside monthly into a separate account.

### 2.4 Payment terms by region (McKay)
US Net 30; Europe Net 60, sometimes Net 60 EOM (effectively up to 90 days). For our B2B interpreting, 30 days is standard — resist 60+ unless ironclad and 25%-safe.

### 2.5 Late-payment law (Samuelsson-Brown)
UK: Late Payment of Commercial Debts (Interest) Act 1998 (statutory interest, 30-day default). IE/ES/PT: EU Directive 2011/7/EU transposed locally. Cite the act on the second/third reminder for legal weight.

### 2.6 The non-payment escalation ladder (McKay, Gebhardt, Samuelsson-Brown — combined)
Invoice → due day 30 → friendly "confirming receipt?" nudge day 35–40 → second notice day 60 → final registered/certified notice day 75 (reference the Act) → email all company addresses day 80 (colleagues see lateness) → phone day 85 ("calling on behalf of Tatkowski") → collections/small-claims day 90 (IE small claims €2,000; UK £5,000; no solicitor) → factual public posting (ProZ Blue Board). Stay polite while they hold your money; post reviews after resolution.

### 2.7 The 9 excuses for late payment (Samuelsson-Brown) — pattern library
Cheque's in the post / arrived after the cheque run / signer is away / need a statement not an invoice / it's on the boss's desk / passed to accounts (who never got it) / lost in post please resend / X has left / accounts are in another building. Hear two → escalate; don't wait for a third.

### 2.8 Credit limits + due diligence before accepting (Samuelsson-Brown, McKay, Gebhardt)
Set a per-client credit limit; stage payments if a job exceeds it. Vet new clients: full legal name, website, physical address, phone; Whois; ProZ Blue Board (~€20/yr); Payment Practices; Experian for large agencies; references. Red flags (Gebhardt): free email only, no phone, no physical address, requests to mail personal info, asking YOU to pay a "membership".

### 2.9 Daily cash hygiene + invoicing (Jenner, Gebhardt)
Know AR/AP at all times; log receipts daily. Invoice weekly not monthly (terms start at invoice date). Big visible TOTAL DUE; explicit due date; client PO ref; logical invoice numbering; a marketing tagline in spare invoice space. (Revolut Business covers our multi-currency invoicing.)

### 2.10 Escrow when offered (Gebhardt)
Always use platform escrow (funds upfront, released on delivery); the cut buys certainty.

## 3. THE CLIENT-ACQUISITION ENGINE

### 3.1 The numbers (McKay, Gebhardt)
~300–500 contacts in year one to build to full-time (McKay); ~1% cold-response rate — expect ~99 no's per yes; marketing stays ~10% of time even when established. Gebhardt: 10–20 cold prospects/week at the start, <10% response, most early leads are agencies.

### 3.2 Kill cold-calling; build the intro engine (Jenner)
Cold calling converts ~2%. Instead: identify the target → find a mutual connection → formally ask for the intro (an honour, not a right) → take both to lunch (you pay) → CC the introducer on the first email → always thank them regardless of outcome. Pre-qualified leads via mutual connections convert far better.

### 3.3 The direct-client acquisition pattern (Jenner) — the playbook to copy
Pick 3–5 target companies in a priority sector → read their industry press → find the right contact → find a mutual connection → ask for a warm intro → pre-arrange a meeting at their convenience → show up prepared → thank contact + introducer → be patient (first project ~5 months out in the example).

### 3.4 Lawyer word-of-mouth is THE channel for legal interpreting (Mikkelson)
"Word of mouth is far more valuable than any other marketing tool … lawyers are a tight-knit group and they often exchange information about interpreters." Do 1–2 excellent jobs for top IE firms, then explicitly ask for referrals; target partners directly. Same dynamic for healthcare (referrals between clinics).

### 3.5 AIDA + the "So What" test (Gebhardt) — fix our copy
AIDA: Attention → Interest (benefits not features) → Desire (emotional/likeability, limited-time offers) → Action (one obvious next step). The "So What" filter: every marketing line must state a client BENEFIT. "30+ languages / ATII-certified / fast" → so what? Rewrite to "one invoice across IE/UK/ES/PT", "accepted by Irish courts and hospitals — no rejection risk", "documents back in 24–48h".

### 3.6 Be a solution provider, not a CV (Jenner, Gebhardt)
No résumés to direct clients — they buy a service, not an employee. Communicate five things: what you do for THEM; relevant industry-targeted experience; why they can trust you; credentials; your specific competitive advantage. Research the company; name a language problem they may not know they have.

### 3.7 Repeat-customer engine (Jenner) — cheapest revenue there is
Follow up within days of delivery (most contractors never do); monthly pipeline check-ins with top clients; exclusive flat rates for high-volume repeats; handwritten thank-you + sticky discount on invoices; small thoughtful gifts. Take call notes, send recap emails, track personal context, adapt to their tools.

### 3.8 Cheap channels (Jenner)
Local/industry press with a story angle (free PR sites, 3–6 short paragraphs); pro bono that opens doors (free school career day → the school's paid work). ~30 min every few months sustains PR.

### 3.9 Templates without laziness (Gebhardt)
3–4 specialty templates (legal/business/general); personalise name, job type in their language, your most relevant past project, your rate. Don't rewrite from scratch (burnout) or paste identically (reads lazy).

## 4. QUALITY & OPERATIONS STANDARDS

### 4.1 The 5 Quality Gaps (Samuelsson-Brown) — audit each B2B onboarding
Expectation vs PM's understanding; PM's understanding vs written spec; spec vs delivery; advertising promise vs delivery; client's prior expectation vs experience. Mitigation: written spec per job, repeat back, confirm in writing.

### 4.2 Client-education scripts (Samuelsson-Brown) — pre-write for inbound
Counter the five classic misconceptions: "tidy up my draft" (often = full retranslation cost; offer proper translation), "5,000 words overnight cheap" (skilled labour), "computer does it now, charge less" (knowledge work, not keystrokes), "just a rough translation" (offer a ≤300-word abstract OR a checked translation), "no input needed" (dialogue is essential).

### 4.3 Translation-purpose intake (Samuelsson-Brown) — classify every quote
Information-extraction / abstract / information / publication (downstream-liability risk) / legal-certified (highest QC, Statement of Truth/Accuracy, footnote untranslatable legal concepts). Drives QC level and price.

### 4.4 The intake checklist (McKay, Samuelsson-Brown) — confirm SalesManager captures it
Subject + your confidence; sample pages; word/page count; deadline + timezone (not "end of day"); format (PDF/Word/HTML/scan/handwriting); TM + discount?; base rate; special instructions; who answers queries; house style; what it's FOR.

### 4.5 Production-rate benchmarks (Samuelsson-Brown, McKay) — capacity planning
~1,500 source words/day/translator sustainable new work; ~1,500 words/hr checking another's work; +30% buffer for QC/formatting. McKay: ~400–600 words/hr, 25–50% non-billable time. Sell within capacity; decline rather than fail.

### 4.6 Be honest about deadlines (Samuelsson-Brown)
"We would rather turn down work than produce poor quality that damages our reputation." Call AHEAD of a deadline, never go silent. If forced to take a tight job, mark "UNCHECKED DRAFT", check next morning, flag changes.

### 4.7 The 8 Canons of court-interpreter ethics (Mikkelson) — adopt as contractor standard
Accuracy; Impartiality & conflict-of-interest disclosure; Confidentiality; Limitations of practice (no legal advice); Protocol & demeanor (same grammatical person); Maintenance of skills; Accurate representation of credentials; Impediments (flag fatigue/inability to hear/terminology gaps). A named standard = quality differentiator + liability protection; quote it in B2B proposals.

### 4.8 On-assignment ethics = non-circumvention (Community Interpreter)
Interpreters "should not seek new business opportunities for [their] own benefit while on assignment"; a mid-assignment offer is accepted only with the contracting provider's formal approval. This industry norm makes our non-circumvention clause enforceable and defensible — reinforces the 5-day rule and "lock interpreter before sharing contact".

### 4.9 Editor/translator QA without warfare (Gebhardt)
Editors annotate WITH rationale (not bare track-changes); translators respond constructively. Prevents the editor-over-corrects-to-justify-fee failure mode on our team.

## 5. THE TERMS-OF-SERVICE TEMPLATE

### 5.1 The 7 clauses to add to every B2B contract (McKay)
1. No claims accepted after X days from invoice (2–4 weeks).
2. Claims limited to the invoice amount (caps downstream liability).
3. The client's TOS isn't in force until we approve it in writing (stops post-hoc terms).
4. Our agreement is with the client only, not their end-client.
5. We retain copyright until the invoice is paid in full (leverage for non-payment).
6. Cancellation: paid for work completed to the cancellation point.
7. We must be given the chance to correct before any payment reduction.

### 5.2 Contract red flags to refuse/negotiate when WE subcontract or sign B2B terms (McKay)
"We pay you only after our end-client pays" (absorbs their credit risk — refuse); broad indemnification (need E&O cover); non-compete (only reasonable with genuine client overlap); no-subcontracting (comply if signed); NDAs (read carefully for legal/financial work); credit/background/bonding (know what you're sharing).

### 5.3 Subcontractor engagement (Gebhardt, Community Interpreter)
A written confirmation of word count, deadline, fee, format carries enough legal weight for routine work. ADD a non-circumvention clause (industry-normed per Community Interpreter). Formalise David's arrangement; per-job engagement notes for translator contractors via SalesManager.

### 5.4 Insurance (Samuelsson-Brown, Medical Interpreter, Routledge PSI)
Professional-indemnity + E&O is standard for self-employed interpreters and a procurement requirement for B2B/public tenders (commonly €1–5M). A sole proprietor's personal assets are exposed to a judgment. Confirm our level; raise before pursuing tenders; cite it as evidence in proposals.

## 6. THE B2B & PARTNERSHIP STRATEGY

### 6.1 Reciprocal-referral network (Tustison)
Most listed agencies don't cover our IE+UK+ES+PT spread under one roof. Pitch reciprocity: we're their in-market certified-translation + court/medical-interpreting subcontractor; they refer in-market work to us, we refer their language pairs out. Target ~10–20 partnerships for surge capacity + referral pipeline. Verified targets in notes: IE (Iota, Mogi, Verbum, Vistatec), PT (ad verbum, AP Portugal, Blue Dimension, BTS, Consenso, Gibberish, Glossarium, HCR, Idioma Global, Inovtrad), plus ES/UK lists.

### 6.2 "Interpreting-focused" is rare (Tustison)
The directory is overwhelmingly translation shops; explicit interpreting focus is uncommon. Our interpreting-lead positioning is a genuine differentiator.

### 6.3 Sell compliance-as-risk-control to healthcare/legal B2B (Medical Interpreter, Mikkelson, Routledge PSI)
The pitch isn't "cheap"; it's "we de-risk your language-access obligation and protect patient/defendant safety with qualified, insured interpreters." Lead with the documented danger of ad-hoc/family interpreting (clinical/legal error). Translate the US language-access mechanism into the IE/UK/ES/PT equality/patient-rights equivalent for market copy.

### 6.4 Procurement readiness (Routledge PSI)
Public/B2B work runs on framework agreements; lowest-price-wins is giving way to quality criteria; ISO 17100/18841 increasingly required; pre-qualification filters on certifications, capacity, references, insurance. Build the evidence pack now.

### 6.5 Division of labour for outreach

## 7. SCALING MATH

### 7.1 Solo → agency steps (Gebhardt)
Incorporate (✓ CRO 803790) → recruit translators (junior, capable, hungry; pay less than you charge; test first jobs; have more than one) → recruit an editor (more grammatically precise; keeps translators honest) → mind the margin.

### 7.2 Margin discipline (Gebhardt)
If the client pays €0.10/word and the translator takes €0.06, the editor must be costed low (e.g. €0.02 for the shorter task) to leave a real margin (~20%). A 60/40 split that eats the whole client price leaves €0 — broken. Keep ≥30–40% gross margin after translator/editor cost.

### 7.3 Tatkowski margin sanity-check (derived from our rates + notes)
IE €29.99/page vs ~$12/page (Vovka) ≈ ~60% gross margin ✓. PT/ES €49.99/page vs estimated €20–25/page translator ≈ 50–60% ✓. B2B interpreting at ~2× interpreter rate (Olga €50/hr → our €150 first hour + €50/30 min) ✓. Healthy provided volume justifies the management overhead.

### 7.4 Billable-hours rate model (McKay)
Required rate = (owner draw + business costs + profit) ÷ billable hours (~1,068/yr after holidays/sick/non-billable). Roughly (€40k + €20k + 20%) ÷ ~1,000 ≈ €72/hr → at ~500 words/hr ≈ €0.14/word ≈ €42/300-word page — broadly consistent with our €29.99–€49.99 spread.

### 7.5 Own the agency identity (Gebhardt, Jenner)
Be forthcoming that we're an agency — "our team / our certified contractors", never faking a one-person shop or a fake "we". Cleaner on liability, less stress, no later credibility mismatch.

### 7.6 Agency-as-talent-pipeline (Conference Interpreting, China)
The Japanese and Chinese elite-interpreter models were built inside agency/institution training programmes. Long-horizon relevance for Tatkowski Systems if we ever build an in-house training/qualification pipeline as a moat.

## 8. MARKET INTELLIGENCE (THE WEDGE)

### 8.1 The PSI outsourcing crisis = our opening (Routledge PSI)
Governments outsourced public-service interpreting to private companies to save money; the text is explicit it "has not proved ideal." Post-2008 cuts "transformed PSI into a commodity … the winner is a cheap service rather than a quality one," and outsourcing is "destroying systems where service providers were once encouraged to use registered and state-certified interpreters" (UK, Netherlands cited). COVID boosted demand. Net: there is documented, structural dissatisfaction with cheap outsourced interpreting — and Tatkowski is the quality-first alternative.

### 8.2 Tseng's 4-stage professionalisation model (Routledge PSI) — where our markets sit
(1) market disorder + no recognition → (2) consensus on aspirations → (3) associations + codes of conduct → (4) code adherence + control of entry. Tatkowski read: IE ~2–3 (ATII, no statute), UK ~3 but eroded by outsourcing, ES ~2 for PSI (jurada mature), PT ~2 developing. We operate as a stage-3/4 professional in markets that are mostly earlier — that's the differentiation.

### 8.3 The interpreter shortage is structural and global (Mikkelson, China)
Court-interpreter shortage is a recurring crisis (Mikkelson); a UN-side practitioner notes "a serious shortage of freelance interpreters on the market" (China). Demand for qualified interpreters routinely outstrips supply, especially at the top of the quality distribution and in less-common languages. Owning a vetted interpreter roster IS the scarce, valuable asset.

### 8.4 Regulatory tailwind: EU Directive 2010/64/EU (Mikkelson, Routledge PSI)
Requires interpreting/translation in criminal proceedings across member states; still unevenly implemented, especially ES/PT — which are professionalising now. Our ES/PT presence is well-timed to ride this.

### 8.5 Healthcare demand mechanism (Medical Interpreter)
Language-access obligations on providers (US Title VI etc.; EU equality/patient-rights equivalents) + patient-safety risk from ad-hoc/family interpreting = durable, compliance-driven demand. Providers are obligated, cost-pressured, and under-enforced — they want reliable, correctly-priced interpreting that de-risks compliance.

### 8.6 ISO as a procurement moat (Routledge PSI)
ISO 17100 (translation) and 18841 (interpreting) are becoming procurement requirements. Our Q3 2026 ISO 17100 pursuit is correctly prioritised; 18841 is the natural follow-on for the interpreting lead product.

### 8.7 Scarcity pricing + the dilution trap (China)
Scarcity carried a 3×-salary "halo"; that premium then drew low-quality entrants. The lesson is paired: price scarce high-stakes work at a premium AND defend it with verifiable quality signals, because the premium itself attracts cheap imitators.

## 9. FORBIDDEN PATTERNS (what the corpus says NOT to do)

1. **Per-hour pricing for translation** (Gebhardt, McKay) — caps earnings, sells time not value, races to the bottom. Per-word/per-page instead. (Per-hour OK only for editing and B2B interpreting time.)
2. **Quoting sight-unseen** (Samuelsson-Brown) — always get sample pages first.
3. **Accepting rush jobs from NEW clients** (Gebhardt) — urgency from an unknown = disorganised upstream = late payment, invented faults, change demands. Decline by default.
4. **Discounting the base rate under pressure** (Jenner) — flex rush/PDF/bonus/non-profit/expired-promo instead; the base holds.
5. **Faking "we" / hiding that we're an agency** (Jenner, Gebhardt) — be forthcoming; cleaner on liability and trust.
6. **Letting any client exceed ~25% of revenue** (Samuelsson-Brown) — existential concentration risk.
7. **Going silent on a slipping deadline** (Samuelsson-Brown) — call AHEAD, always in writing.
8. **"Just tidy up my draft" / "just a rough translation" at a cut rate** (Samuelsson-Brown) — offer a proper translation or a clearly-marked abstract; don't absorb full work at a discount.
9. **Letting interpreters solicit our clients on assignment** (Community Interpreter) — non-circumvention is an industry ethic; enforce it.
10. **Single-interpreter quotes for long/conference sessions** (Mikkelson, Setton & Dawrant) — team interpreting is the professional standard; quote it.
11. **Absorbing inflation silently year after year** (Jenner) — annual 3–5% raise, inform don't ask.
12. **Cold-calling as the primary acquisition channel** (Jenner) — ~2% conversion; build the warm-intro engine instead.
13. **Chasing prospects who said no, or poaching clients who already have a good provider** (Jenner) — wasted effort, bad form.
